# Investment philosophy

## Timeline and return target

I hold for **2 to 3 years**. Still long-term, but tighter than a literal
buy-and-hold-forever stance. Over that window I'm looking for **5× to
10× from entry** — asymmetric upside, not steady compounding. Names
that can't plausibly clear that bar are either defensive anchors or
not interesting.

## Sector focus

The whole portfolio sits inside one secular bet: the **AI super-cycle**.
Concretely that's the build-out around AI training and inference —
data centers, networking, power and cooling, materials, chips, memory,
optical interconnect, fab and process equipment.

I want exposure to the picks-and-shovels layer more than the
application layer. Foundational compute and the supply chain that
feeds it should compound regardless of which model lab or AI product
wins the next round.

## What I'm looking for

- **Unpopular with retail.** Hidden gems that haven't been bid up yet.
  Being early is half the edge.
- **Credible AI repositioning.** Mature businesses pivoting into the
  cycle with real customers and real capex, not slide-deck stories.
- **Asymmetric setups.** Downside is bounded by existing business value;
  upside is unlocked by AI-cycle exposure the market hasn't priced in.

## What I avoid

- **Mega-cap AI names as 5-10× plays.** NVDA, AVGO, MSFT and the
  obvious large caps are defensive anchors — useful for a 2-3% sleeve,
  not the engine of the portfolio.
- **Saturated sentiment.** High retail crowdedness (heavy social
  volume, broad analyst coverage, deep institutional ownership) fails
  the hidden-gem screen.
- **Short-term trades.** I'm not playing earnings runs, options gamma,
  or technical breakouts.

## How I want Claude to use this

When summarizing a ticker or writing a thesis card:

- Judge it explicitly against these criteria — timeline, return
  potential, AI exposure, retail crowdedness.
- Surface smaller-cap AI exposure (memory, optical, power, materials,
  fab equipment) more readily than the obvious large caps.
- Use institutional ownership %, sentiment volume, 13F activity, and
  analyst coverage breadth as proxies for retail-crowdedness.
- When recommending position sizing, distinguish defensive anchors
  (small sleeve) from higher-conviction hidden gems (larger
  allocation), and respect single-name concentration caps.
